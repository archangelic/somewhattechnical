I'm Not "Out" at Work

by Mallory Hancock

June 24 2016, 1:26 PM

I think a lot about my safety. I am a trans-feminine, hairy, non-binary, fat person. I don’t fit neatly into a mold and safety is always a concern. After Orlando, I fear even more. I am hyper-aware when I am in public because I am concerned for my physical safety. I am afraid even in a “friendly” city like Portland, OR.

Beyond that, my stability and ability to meet basic needs are threatened by systematic discrimination. I lost a job I loved in January of this year, and spent the next 5 months looking for a job. I did not use my affirming name. I did not present femme in any way. I was afraid that I would not be considered for jobs if I did, and I felt I had reason to to believe that. I interviewed once as Mallory. I did not get that job, despite the interview going incredibly well. I can’t help but feel like I wasn’t hired because I was trans.

I have been working in tech support for nearly four years, and being in cishet male-dominated spaces is common for me. I know what is said between men in the industry, as I often present as a cishet male. I know that I might be passed over simply for being trans. It’s hard enough to get the industry to shift to hiring cis women, let alone me and my trans siblings.

I finally got hired doing tech support for a small company. I am Michael there. I think they have gathered that I am at least not-straight, but I am still a man in their eyes. I will keep it this way because I need my stability, and I need to have money to do that.

Which brings me to my point: as LGBTQ+ people, safety, both physically and financially, should be considered vital. Being out is a privilege for so many of us. Visibility and the ability to live out in the open is a fantastic goal for us, but we must remember we are far from reaching that goal and our community should equally support people who can’t or choose not to be out.

I want to let all of you know that if you decide to blend in because it is safe, I see you and I love you and I hope the community at large does as well.

You are a beautiful and valuable person even if you are not out.
Your safety is paramount.

I want to hear from trans people in tech. Let’s share stories and support.
